# AI's Economics Don't Make Sense

Source: <https://www.wheresyoured.at/ais-economics-dont-make-sense/>

## 1. AI subscriptions were never financially viable

AI companies sold flat-rate subscriptions ($20/month, $200/month, etc.) while many users consumed far more in compute than they paid for.

- Copilot users allegedly cost Microsoft more than their subscription fees.
- Heavy users of coding tools like Claude Code may consume hundreds or thousands of dollars of GPU compute monthly.
- AI firms obscured the real costs behind abstractions like “messages,” “requests,” or “credits.”

LLM inference costs scale directly with usage, unlike traditional SaaS products where marginal costs are relatively stable.

## 2. Token-based billing is inevitable

GitHub Copilot’s move to token billing is presented as a turning point:

- Instead of “unlimited” usage, users now effectively pay for consumed compute.
- Users reacted negatively because they had been trained to think of AI as unlimited.

Predicts:

- More AI products will abandon flat-rate pricing.
- Enterprise customers are already being pushed toward token pricing.
- Once consumer AI apps fully switch, the “AI hype cycle” could weaken substantially.

## 3. AI ROI is unproven

A major section questions whether businesses actually gain enough productivity to justify AI spending.

- Companies are spending enormous amounts on AI tokens.
- Executives often cannot measure concrete productivity gains.
- Studies on AI return-on-investment have produced weak evidence.

The author is particularly skeptical of coding assistants:

- Even if developers “ship faster,” token costs may offset savings.
- Large teams could easily spend six or seven figures annually on AI inference.
- Investors may eventually scrutinize those costs more aggressively.

## 4. AI systems are unreliable in ways that become expensive

The article stresses that LLMs:

- hallucinate,
- fail unpredictably,
- require repeated prompting,
- and can waste large amounts of compute without producing useful results.

Under subscription pricing, these flaws feel tolerable because retries appear “free.” Under token billing, every failure has a visible monetary cost.

The author argues that many users would have rejected AI products much earlier if they had seen the actual per-task expense from the beginning

## 5. AI infrastructure economics may also be broken

The latter half expands from software economics to AI infrastructure.

The article argues that AI datacenters:

- cost billions to build,
- rely heavily on debt,
- require near-perfect utilization,
- and often generate thin or negative margins after depreciation and financing costs.

The author presents rough financial models suggesting:

- even fully utilized GPU datacenters may struggle financially,
- projects depend heavily on a few giant customers like OpenAI and Anthropic,
- and the economics become dangerous if demand weakens or pricing falls.

A large section criticizes the economics of the Stargate Abilene project as an example of massive capital expenditure tied to uncertain long-term returns.
